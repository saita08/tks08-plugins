# The Discipline of Translating Principles Under a Limit

A full institution — a constitution, a set of working principles — is written at length on purpose. Each principle states a value and the reasoning that makes it true, and that reasoning is what lets the principle govern a situation nobody enumerated in advance. Translating such a document into a service with a tight length limit puts pressure on exactly that length, and there is a right way and a wrong way to relieve it.

## The betrayal to avoid

The wrong way is the tempting one: replace a principle's statement-of-a-value with a couple of concrete examples of it. It reads well, it is short, and it fits the box. And it destroys the principle. A reader — human or model — treats listed examples as the definition and stops applying the principle where the situation does not match the list. The generality that made the principle worth having is gone, and what remains is a rule about three specific cases masquerading as a value.

This is the single failure this discipline exists to prevent. A translation that fits the limit by turning principles into example-lists has not compressed the institution; it has replaced it with a weaker thing that happens to be shorter.

## The right way to shorten

Relieve length pressure in this order, stopping as soon as the translation fits:

1. **Cut the scaffolding, keep the values.** A full document carries meta-discussion (what the document is, how it should be amended), transitions, and restatements. These go first; they are the least load-bearing and their loss costs no principle.

2. **Tighten each value's statement.** State the value in fewer words while keeping it a general value. "Before designing, find out how the world already solves the problem, because conventions have survived requirements this project has not yet met" can tighten toward "Prefer established, battle-tested solutions over bespoke ones; deviate only deliberately." Shorter, still general, still reaches the unforeseen case. This is compression done right: the value survives, only the words shrink.

3. **Drop the reasoning clause before the value.** If tightening is not enough, a principle can keep its value-statement and lose the *because* clause that explains it. The instruction survives; only its justification is cut. This costs something — an instruction with its reasoning is more robust than a bare one — so it comes after tightening, not before.

4. **Cut whole principles, and record which.** When even that is not enough, drop entire principles rather than hollowing every remaining one into a slogan. A document of ten intact principles serves better than twenty gutted ones. Choose what to cut by importance to the user's actual practice, not by which compresses most conveniently.

## Report what the limit cost

Whenever a hard limit forces content out — step 4, and sometimes step 3 — the loss must be visible. The user is owed a plain statement of which principles were dropped or which reasoning was stripped, and why (the target's limit). This turns a silent degradation into a decision the user can act on: re-target to a service with more room, shorten the source themselves, split the institution across a service's multiple fields, or accept the loss knowingly. A translation that quietly drops a third of the constitution and presents the rest as complete has lied about what it delivered.

## The test for any translated principle

Read the translated line and ask: does this still tell the reader *how to behave in a case I did not list here*? If yes, the principle survived translation. If it only tells them how to behave in the specific cases named, it has decayed into an example-list and must be restated as a value or cut outright. The length was never the substance; the generality was.
